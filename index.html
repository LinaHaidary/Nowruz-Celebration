Nowroz: Celebrating
the Solar New Year

Scroll Down

Nowruz, marking the Persian New Year, is deeply tied to the solar year and the vernal equinox, around March 20th or 21st. Rooted in Zoroastrianism and predating Islamic influence, Nowruz celebrates cosmic renewal and natural rebirth. It aligns with the Hijri Shamsi calendar, which begins its year with the vernal equinox. This calendar, based on the tropical year of approximately 365.24219 days. Nowruz's precise timing underscores its historical and cultural significance, emphasizing humanity's connection to nature's rhythms.

The first day of the Hijri Shamsi calendar is celebrated as Nowruz in various countries including Iran, Afghanistan, Tajikistan, Uzbekistan, Turkmenistan, Azerbaijan, Kurdish regions in Iraq, Turkey, and Syria, India (Parsi community), Albania, and Turkey (Alevi and Kurdish populations). The celebrations encompass a wide range of traditions: the Haft-Seen table, home cleaning, customs and farmers' festivals, traditional foods and bonfire jumping, communal gatherings, music, and traditional sports. Additionally, there are associations with freedom, featuring bonfires and dances, prayers and feasts, and celebrating the arrival of spring.

Traditional Tajik music, dance, and crafts are integral to its cultural identity, celebrated during festivals and social gatherings.

Traditions In These Countries

Afghanistan New Year

In Afghanistan, Nowruz, also known as "Nawroz," is celebrated with great enthusiasm and is marked by various traditional customs. Preparation begins with thorough cleaning of homes, symbolizing purification for the new year. One unique Afghan tradition is the preparation of "Haft Mewa," a fruit salad made from seven different dried fruits and nuts, and set up the Haft-Seen table symbolizing hopes and blessings for the new year. In the city of Mazar-e-Sharif, the "Mela-e Gul-e Surkh" (Red Flower Festival) coincides with Nowruz, celebrating the blooming of tulips. Another notable event is the "Jahenda Bala Ceremony," where a special flag is raised at the Blue Mosque, symbolizing peace and unity. Buzkashi, a traditional equestrian sport, is also associated with Nowruz celebrations, particularly in northern Afghanistan.

Iran New Year

In Iran, Nowruz is the most significant holiday, deeply rooted in cultural traditions. Iranians start preparing for Nowruz weeks in advance, cleaning their homes, buying new clothes, and purchasing special foods and sweets. The "Haft-Seen" table is elaborately decorated with seven symbolic items representing various aspects of life and nature, such as rebirth, love, and prosperity. Nowruz is celebrated with public events, including "Chaharshanbe Suri", celebrated on the last Wednesday before Nowruz, involves jumping over bonfires to symbolically shed past year's misfortunes. "Sizdah Bedar" marks the end of Nowruz festivities with outdoor picnics. Visiting relatives, exchanging gifts, wearing new clothes, and sprouting seeds are common customs during Nowruz in Iran.

Tajikistan New Year

In Tajikistan, Nowruz known as "Navruz" in Tajikistan, is one of the most important and widely celebrated festivals in the country. Nowruz celebrations blend ancient Zoroastrian traditions with local customs, fostering communal harmony and connection with nature. Preparations include cleaning homes and preparing traditional dishes like "Sumalak" (sweet paste made from germinated wheat). Public gatherings feature music, dancing, and traditional sports like wrestling. Folk music and dances highlight the country's rich cultural heritage. Many Tajiks visit sacred sites and nature spots to offer prayers and celebrate the renewal of life. Exchanging gifts and providing "Eidi" to children strengthen familial and community bonds during Nowruz.
